# Discovery Frameworks Reference

*Used by: discovery-prep/SKILL.md — Step 2.5 (Upfront Contract) and Step 5 (Pain Questions)*

---

## 1. SPIN Selling

**Purpose:** Structure pain discovery to build urgency naturally. Never jump to implications before situation and problem are established.

| Question Type | What it uncovers | Example |
|--------------|-----------------|---------|
| **Situation** | Current state facts | "Walk me through what your [process] looks like today." |
| **Problem** | Explicit difficulties | "What's the hardest part about [process] right now?" |
| **Implication** | Consequences of the problem | "If that keeps happening, what does that mean for your team / your numbers?" |
| **Need-Payoff** | Value of solving it | "If you could [fix the problem], what would that be worth to you?" |

**Rule:** Never skip to Implication without a confirmed Problem. The buyer must say the problem out loud before you ask about impact.

**Implication questions that work:**
- "How does that affect the rest of the team when [problem] happens?"
- "What's the downstream impact on [metric] when [problem] occurs?"
- "How often does this happen — and what does each occurrence cost?"
- "What have you had to deprioritize because of this?"

## 2. Gap Selling

**Purpose:** Establish the gap between current state and desired future state before discussing your solution. Root cause first.

**Three-part structure:**
1. **Current State** — where they are now (facts, not your interpretation)
2. **Root Cause** — why the problem exists (dig past symptoms)
3. **Future State** — where they want to be (their words, not your pitch)

**Key principle:** The gap IS the sale. Make it vivid before you introduce any capability.

**Root cause questions:**
- "Why do you think that's happening?"
- "Is this a people problem, a process problem, or a tooling problem?"
- "What have you tried to solve this before? What worked, what didn't?"

**Future state questions:**
- "If this was working perfectly 6 months from now, what would that look like?"
- "What would you be able to do that you can't do today?"

## 3. Sandler Pain Funnel

**Purpose:** Move from surface pain → business impact → personal/emotional impact. Deals are won at Level 3.

| Level | Focus | Questions |
|-------|-------|-----------|
| **Level 1 — Surface** | Technical symptoms | "Tell me more about the problem. What exactly breaks?" |
| **Level 2 — Business Impact** | Revenue, cost, risk, time | "What does that mean for the business? What's the cost of this?" |
| **Level 3 — Personal/Emotional** | Champion's career, stress, credibility | "How does this affect you personally? Is this something that keeps you up at night?" |

**Rule:** Most sellers stop at Level 1. Champions are won at Level 3.

**Level 3 questions (use carefully — only when rapport is strong):**
- "How does this reflect on your team when it happens?"
- "Is this something your leadership notices?"
- "What would it mean for you personally if this was solved by Q[X]?"

## 4. Upfront Contract

**Purpose:** Set expectations at the start of every call. Eliminates "I need to think about it" endings. Normalizes a "no" so you can earn a real "yes."

**Template (adapt to meeting type):**

> "[Name], I want to make sure this is a good use of your time today.
> Here's what I was thinking: I'll spend the first [5–10 minutes] understanding [their situation/challenge].
> Then I'll share [specific thing — a few examples / how others are solving this / one specific capability].
> At the end, one of two things will be true: either this makes sense to explore further — in which case we'll figure out the right next step — or it doesn't make sense, and we'll both know that. Either outcome is fine.
> Does that work for you?"

**Why it works:**
- Gives the buyer control
- Removes the awkward "I'll think about it" at the end
- Sets up the close naturally: "Based on what we covered, does this make sense to explore further?"

## 5. The 60/40 Rule

**Purpose:** Track talk time. In a great discovery, the buyer speaks 60%+ of the time.

**Tracking heuristic:**
- If you're talking more than the buyer — stop and ask a question
- Count your sentences per turn. If > 3 → you're pitching, not discovering

**Recovery phrase when you've been talking too long:**
> "I've been talking a lot — let me stop. What's your reaction to what you've heard so far?"

**Signs you've violated 60/40:**
- You're explaining features
- You're filling silence with more words
- The buyer gives 1-sentence answers
- You leave the call without a direct quote from them about their pain

## 6. Silence as a Tool

**Purpose:** After an implication question, silence is productive. The buyer is processing. Do not fill it.

**Rule:** After any implication or impact question, wait at least 5 seconds before speaking.

**What silence signals to the buyer:** You're confident. You expect a real answer. You're not desperate.

**What to do in silence:**
- Write down what they last said
- Look expectant, not anxious
- If silence stretches past 10 seconds, use: "Take your time — I want to make sure I understand."

**Never do this:**
- Rephrase the question (you just gave them an escape)
- Answer your own question
- Jump to a clarifying question before they've answered

## 7. AECR In-Meeting Objection Handling

**Purpose:** Handle objections raised during discovery or demos — NOT cold calls (use AAA framework in cold-call-framework.md for cold calls).

**Cross-reference:** For cold call objections ("I'm not interested", "We have something"), see `.claude/skills/write-outreach/references/cold-call-framework.md` — AAA (Acknowledge → Ask → Act).

**AECR Steps:**

| Step | What you do | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **A — Acknowledge** | Validate the objection. Never argue. | "That's a fair concern." / "I hear that." |
| **E — Empathize** | Show you've seen this before | "A lot of [their title]s I talk to start there." |
| **C — Clarify** | Ask a question to understand the real objection | "When you say [objection], are you saying [X] or [Y]?" |
| **R — Reframe** | Offer a different lens — don't sell | "What I've seen is [reframe]. Does that change how you're thinking about it?" |

**Common in-meeting objections:**

| Objection | AECR Response |
|-----------|--------------|
| "We're happy with [current tool]" | A: Fair. E: Most teams I talk to felt the same initially. C: "What would have to be true for you to explore alternatives?" R: "The teams that switched weren't unhappy — they just outgrew it." |
| "We don't have budget for this" | A: Understood. E: Timing is always a factor. C: "Is this a budget question or a priority question?" R: "The ROI conversation usually changes the budget one." |
| "We're evaluating [competitor]" | A: Makes sense. E: Good to compare options. C: "What's your primary criteria for the evaluation?" R: "We'd love to make sure you're comparing apples to apples on [key differentiator]." |

## 8. Signs of Great Discovery vs. Signs You Rushed

### Signs of Great Discovery (5 indicators)
1. You have at least 2 direct quotes from the buyer describing their pain in their own words
2. You know the business impact of the problem (time, money, risk, or competitive position)
3. You've established at least 1 implication (what happens downstream if nothing changes)
4. You have a named next step with a date agreed on the call
5. The buyer asked at least 1 question about how you solve their specific problem

### Signs You Rushed (5 red flags)
1. You left the call without a direct pain quote
2. You pitched before they confirmed a problem
3. You ran out of time and skipped the close
4. The buyer agreed to "think about it" with no date
5. You filled all the silences — they never had to think
