# Challenger Messaging Framework

*Used by: write-outreach/SKILL.md — when Classic route is selected or buyer appears complacent*

---

## When to Use Challenger vs. Default SPARK

| Situation | Use | Why |
|-----------|-----|-----|
| Classic route + CxO audience | **Challenger** | CxOs respond to insight, not features |
| Complacent buyer ("we're fine") | **Challenger** | Need to reframe before you can sell |
| Competitive displacement | **Challenger** | Reframe the status quo as the real risk |
| Inbound, already interested buyer | **SPARK** | They want info, not disruption |
| SDR cold outreach (first touch) | **SPARK** | Build enough curiosity to get the meeting |
| Discovery or follow-up | **SPARK + SPIN** | You're in listening mode, not teaching mode |

**Rule:** SPARK picks the hook and opens the door. Challenger sequences the body when you're in Classic route or writing a business case.

---

## The 6-Step Challenger Teaching Sequence

### Step 1 — Warmer
*Establish common ground. Show you understand their world.*

> "Companies like yours in [industry] are dealing with [common challenge]. You're probably seeing [specific symptom]."

**Goal:** Make them feel seen. This is NOT your product pitch — it's about their world.

**Template:**
> "{{COMPANY_NAME}}'s customers in [their industry] are navigating [market shift/trend]. Most [their job title]s we talk to are dealing with [problem symptom]."

---

### Step 2 — Reframe
*Introduce a new way of seeing the problem they already accept.*

> "What most [their title]s don't realize is that [root cause] — not [what they think the problem is] — is what's actually holding them back."

**Goal:** Challenge their assumption about the problem. Not their intelligence — their framing.

**Template:**
> "What we've found is that [the problem they think they have] is usually a symptom of [actual root cause]. The teams that fix [symptom] and leave [root cause] in place end up right back here in 12 months."

---

### Step 3 — Rational Drowning
*Hit them with data/evidence that the problem is bigger than they thought.*

> "Here's what the data shows: [companies/teams in your situation] are [specific measurable consequence]. Left unchecked, [consequence compounds]."

**Goal:** Make the cost of inaction concrete and undeniable. Use their metrics if possible.

**Template:**
> "Teams managing [their current approach] typically spend [X hours/dollars] per [unit of time] on [problem area]. When we map that across a [their team size] team over a year, that's [calculated cost]."

**Note:** Never invent numbers. Only use metrics from verified proof points (proof-points.md or live research).

---

### Step 4 — Emotional Impact
*Connect the rational problem to something personal — career, team morale, competitive position.*

> "And the teams that don't solve this aren't just losing [metric] — they're losing [competitive position / talent / credibility with leadership]."

**Goal:** Activate motivation beyond ROI. The buyer needs a personal reason to act.

**Template:**
> "The [their title]s who solve this don't just hit their [metric] — they're the ones who get the resources, the headcount, and the organizational trust to move faster next quarter."

---

### Step 5 — New Way
*Introduce the category of solution — not your product yet.*

> "The [their title]s who've broken through this have done it by [new approach/capability]. Not by [old approach] — that's what created the problem."

**Goal:** Create a new category in their mind before you name your product.

**Template:**
> "The teams that have cracked this shifted from [old approach] to [new approach]. That shift is what's enabling [desired outcome they want]."

---

### Step 6 — Your Solution
*Now name {{PRODUCT_NAME}} and connect it directly to Steps 1-5.*

> "That's exactly what {{PRODUCT_NAME}} was built for. [One sentence on specific capability]. [One proof point from a similar company]."

**Goal:** By now, your solution is the answer to a problem THEY defined, not a pitch you invented.

**Template:**
> "{{PRODUCT_NAME}} [specific capability] so that [specific outcome from Step 4]. [Customer] went from [before state] to [after state] in [timeframe]."

---

## Common Mistakes to Avoid

| Mistake | Why it fails | Instead |
|---------|-------------|---------|
| Skipping to Step 6 | Feels like a pitch — no insight delivered | Run all 6 steps |
| Using Challenger for inbound | They already believe — you'll annoy them | Use SPARK for inbound |
| Inventing metrics in Step 3 | Kills credibility instantly | Only use verified proof points |
| Making Step 4 generic | "You'll be more productive" is not emotional | Specific to their role and situation |
| Putting your product in Step 5 | Collapses the sequence | Step 5 is category, Step 6 is product |
